###### Abstract

No-arbitrage asset pricing characterizes valuation through the existence of equivalent martingale measures relative to a filtration and a class of admissible trading strategies. In practice, pricing is performed across multiple asset classes driven by economic variables that are only partially spanned by traded instruments, raising a structural question: does there exist a single admissible information structure under which all traded assets can be jointly priced as martingales?. We treat the filtration as an endogenous object constrained by admissibility and time-ordering, rather than as an exogenous primitive. For any finite collection of assets, whenever martingale pricing is feasible under some admissible filtration, it is already feasible under a canonical minimal filtration generated by the asset prices themselves; these pricing-sufficient filtrations are unique up to null sets and stable under restriction and aggregation when a common pricing measure exists. Our main result shows that this local compatibility does not extend globally: with three independent unspanned finite-variation drivers, there need not exist any admissible filtration and equivalent measure under which all assets are jointly martingales. The obstruction is sharp (absent with one driver and compatible pairwise with two) and equivalent to failure of admissible dynamic completeness. We complement the theory with numerical diagnostics based on discrete-time Doob‚ÄìMeyer decompositions, illustrating how admissible information structures suppress predictable components, while inadmissible filtrations generate systematic predictability.

## 1 Introduction

The fundamental theorem of asset pricing characterizes the absence of arbitrage through the
existence of an equivalent martingale measure relative to a filtration and a notion of
admissible trading. Beginning with the seminal work of Harrison and Kreps
[harrison1979martingales] and Harrison and Pliska [harrison1981martingales], and
extended to general semimartingale models by Delbaen and Schachermayer
[delbaen1994general], this framework provides the foundation of modern
continuous-time asset pricing. A defining feature of the theory is that martingale pricing
is inherently conditional on both the information structure and the admissibility constraints
imposed on trading strategies.

In the classical formulation, the filtration is treated as an exogenous primitive. Pricing,
hedging, and questions of market completeness are studied conditional on a fixed information
structure, and market incompleteness is typically reflected in the non-uniqueness of
equivalent martingale measures
[duffie2001dynamic, karatzas1998brownian]. A large literature analyzes the implications of
this multiplicity for valuation, hedging, and risk sharing while maintaining the filtration
as given. Closely related work in the theory of enlargement of filtrations studies how
martingale and semimartingale properties change when a fixed filtration is refined
[jeulin1980semi, jeulin1985grossissements]. These approaches provide powerful tools for
analyzing information effects, but they do not address whether distinct locally valid
information structures can be made globally compatible under admissibility and
non-anticipativity.

This paper adopts a complementary perspective. Rather than taking the information structure
as fixed, we treat it as an endogenous object constrained by admissibility and time-ordering,
and ask whether there exists a single admissible filtration under which martingale pricing
can be achieved simultaneously for all admissible assets. This question is not addressed by
standard incompleteness results, which presuppose a common filtration from the outset, nor by
the enlargement-of-filtrations literature, which studies refinements of a given filtration
rather than the compatibility of distinct minimal ones.

Our analysis begins locally. For any finite group of traded assets, we show that whenever
martingale pricing is feasible under some admissible filtration, it is already feasible under
a canonical reduced filtration generated by the asset prices themselves. These
pricing-sufficient filtrations are minimal, unique up to null sets, and determined entirely
by no-arbitrage and admissibility. They support conditional risk-neutral valuation, are stable
under restriction of the asset set, and remain compatible under pairwise aggregation when a
common equivalent martingale measure exists.

The main contribution of the paper is to show that such local and pairwise compatibility does
not, in general, extend globally. Under admissibility constraints, time-ordering, and the
presence of multiple independent unspanned finite-variation drivers, there exists no
admissible filtration and no equivalent probability measure under which all asset prices are
jointly local martingales. The obstruction is sharp and minimal: with a single unspanned
driver global martingale pricing is possible; with two independent drivers pairwise
compatibility can be maintained; and failure arises for the first time with three drivers. We
further show that this non-existence result is equivalent to the failure of admissible dynamic
completeness, thereby placing the obstruction squarely within the classical no-arbitrage
framework.

To complement the theoretical analysis, the paper includes numerical illustrations in a
controlled simulated environment. These illustrations are not empirical tests. Rather, they
provide constructive diagnostics based on discrete-time Doob‚ÄìMeyer decompositions, showing
how admissible information structures induce only negligible predictable finite-variation
components, while inadmissible enlargements of the filtration (violating admissibility or
time-ordering) generate systematic predictability. The simulations visualize the mechanism
underlying the theoretical obstruction and clarify why pricing-consistent conditioning cannot
be extended arbitrarily, even when additional variables appear statistically informative.

## 2 Related Literature

The results of this paper relate to several well-established strands of the literature in
mathematical finance and stochastic analysis, including the theory of no-arbitrage pricing
and equivalent martingale measures, the role of filtrations and information in asset
pricing, incomplete markets and dynamic completeness, stochastic discount factors and limits
of arbitrage, and stochastic control under partial information. While the present
contribution builds on these frameworks, it addresses a question that is not treated
directly in the existing literature: the existence and compatibility of admissible
information structures supporting martingale pricing when the filtration itself is treated
as endogenous.

The modern theory of arbitrage-free pricing characterizes asset prices through the
existence of equivalent martingale measures relative to a given filtration and a notion of
admissible trading. Foundational results were established by Harrison and Kreps
[harrison1979martingales] and Harrison and Pliska [harrison1981martingales], and
extended to general semimartingale models by Delbaen and Schachermayer
[delbaen1994general]. Comprehensive treatments of the theory and its
implications for pricing and hedging in continuous time are given in
[karatzas1998brownian, shiryaev1999essentials, duffie2001dynamic]. A common feature of
this literature is that the filtration is taken as an exogenous primitive, and questions
of pricing, hedging, and completeness are studied conditional on this fixed information
structure.

The dependence of martingale properties on information has been studied systematically in
the theory of enlargement of filtrations, initiated by Jeulin [jeulin1980semi] and
Jeulin and Yor [jeulin1985grossissements], and further developed in
[jacod1979calcul, protter2005stochastic]. This literature characterizes how
semimartingale and martingale properties are affected by progressive or initial
enlargements of a given filtration and provides conditions under which no-arbitrage,
martingale representations, and stability properties are preserved. Applications include
models with insider information, asymmetric information, and anticipation; see, for
example, [amini2016insider, imkeller2013enlargement]. The present paper differs
fundamentally in perspective. Rather than studying refinements of a fixed filtration, we
study whether there exists any admissible filtration that can simultaneously support
martingale pricing for multiple asset groups whose locally pricing-sufficient filtrations
may be incompatible. The obstruction identified here does not arise from a particular
enlargement procedure, but from the non-existence of a common admissible refinement.

A closely related strand of the literature concerns incomplete markets and the resulting
non-uniqueness of equivalent martingale measures
[harrison1981martingales]. In such settings, incompleteness is usually
attributed to insufficient spanning of sources of uncertainty by traded assets. A large
body of work studies pricing and hedging under this multiplicity, including
super-replication [delbaen2002coherent], utility-based and indifference pricing
[hodges1989optimal, carmona2009indifference], and convex risk-measure approaches
[follmer2002convex, delbaen2002coherent]; see also [frittelli2004risk] for links
between pricing and risk measures. In contrast, the present paper does not fix a filtration
and then analyze multiplicity of martingale measures within it. Instead, it shows that
under admissibility and time-ordering, the relevant filtration itself may be asset-dependent,
and failure of global martingale pricing may result from incompatibility between locally
pricing-sufficient filtrations even when local and pairwise martingale measures exist.

The paper also connects to the literature on dynamic completeness, spanning, and the
martingale representation property. Under a fixed filtration, classical results link
(dynamic) completeness to uniqueness of equivalent martingale measures and to the ability
to replicate contingent claims; see, for example, [karatzas1998brownian, duffie2001dynamic]
and the martingale representation discussion in [revuz1999continuous]. More generally,
completeness in diffusion models is characterized by rank conditions on volatility
coefficients and related spanning criteria [karatzas1991martingale, oksendal2003stochastic].
The present paper complements this literature by isolating an obstruction that arises even
prior to questions of uniqueness: admissibility and time-ordering may prevent the existence
of any single filtration under which global martingale pricing holds, and we show that this
is equivalent to failure of *admissible* dynamic completeness.

A further related literature studies stochastic discount factors (state-price deflators) and
limits of arbitrage. The representation of asset prices by stochastic discount factors is
standard in both discrete and continuous time; see, e.g., [duffie2001dynamic, cochrane2005asset].
However, existence, characterization, and aggregation of such kernels depend delicately on
admissibility, market frictions, and segmentation. Classic discussions of limits to
arbitrage and segmentation include [shleifer1997limits] and [gromb2002limits], while
models emphasizing funding and intermediation constraints include [brunnermeier2009market]
and [he2012intermediary]. Demand-based and segmented-market pricing mechanisms that
produce asset-class-specific kernels are studied in [garleanu2011demand].
Our results are orthogonal to equilibrium modeling: the obstruction identified here arises
within the no-arbitrage framework before specifying preferences, market clearing, or
institutional microstructure, and it provides a structural explanation for why
asset-class-specific pricing kernels can persist even absent explicit segmentation assumptions.

Finally, the analysis is related to the literature on stochastic control and filtering under
partial information, where agents optimize dynamically using noisy or incomplete observations.
Classical references include [liptser1977statistics, elliott1997hidden] and treatments in
continuous-time control include [pham2009continuous]. In these models, the filtration is
fixed ex ante and filtering theory is used to construct sufficient statistics for
optimization or equilibrium analysis. The present paper addresses a different question: it
characterizes which information structures are admissible for martingale pricing itself,
independently of optimization objectives or equilibrium beliefs.

Taken together, the contribution of this paper can be viewed as clarifying a gap between
existing frameworks. The filtration enlargement literature studies refinements of a given
information structure [jeulin1980semi, jeulin1985grossissements]; the incomplete markets
literature studies multiplicity of pricing measures under a fixed filtration
[duffie2001dynamic]; the dynamic completeness literature studies spanning
relative to a fixed information set [karatzas1998brownian]; and the partial information
literature studies decision making under exogenously specified information constraints
[liptser1977statistics, elliott1997hidden]. In contrast, this paper studies the
existence and compatibility of admissible information structures supporting martingale
pricing. The main result shows that under admissibility and time-ordering constraints, such
structures need not admit a global refinement, and that this failure is equivalent to the
failure of admissible dynamic completeness. This places the obstruction within the
classical theory of arbitrage-free pricing while isolating a structural limitation that is
not made explicit when the filtration is treated as fixed.

neutralized through admissible trading in SAS^{A}.
This condition is standard in the theory of incomplete markets. Time-scale separation is imposed through a conditional predictability restriction.

###### Definition 3.11 (Time-scale separation).

The driver process YY is said to be *slow relative to* SAS^{A} if, for every equivalent
local martingale measure ‚Ñö\mathbb{Q} under which SAS^{A} is a local martingale with respect
to some admissible filtration, the Doob‚ÄìMeyer decomposition of SAS^{A} relative to the
enlarged filtration ùîΩ‚à®œÉ‚Äã(Y)\mathbb{F}\vee\sigma(Y) contains a predictable finite-variation
component that is measurable with respect to œÉ‚Äã(Y)\sigma(Y).

The terminology ‚Äùslow‚Äù is used in an informational sense: it refers to drivers whose
realizations become predictable relative to asset prices under filtration enlargement,
rather than to an explicit asymptotic separation of physical time scales.

unspanned state variables induces predictable finite-variation components in individual
asset prices that cannot be neutralized by admissible trading strategies.
The results of Sections¬†[3.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS4 "3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") and¬†[3.5](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS5 "3.5 Dynamic Completeness and Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") are conditional on this
assumption and do not claim that time-scale separation holds universally.

This condition captures the idea that conditioning on YY introduces predictable drift
components into asset prices that are not present under smaller filtrations.

following lemma.

### 3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing

This section presents the main non-existence result of the paper. It shows that, under the
structural constraints introduced in Sections¬†[3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Local Existence and Stability of Pricing-Sufficient Filtrations ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") and¬†[3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS3 "3.3 Time-Scale Separation, Unspanned Drivers, and Admissibility ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose"), local and pairwise martingale

uncertainty are present.

### 3.5 Dynamic Completeness and Global Martingale Pricing

This section places the obstruction result of Section¬†[3.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS4 "3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") within the
classical theory of dynamic completeness.
The main result shows that the non-existence of a global admissible martingale pricing
structure is equivalent to failure of admissible dynamic completeness.
This equivalence clarifies the precise scope of the impossibility result and situates it
within established asset-pricing theory.

We work under the standing assumptions of
Sections¬†[3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS1 "3.1 Information Structures, Admissibility, and Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose")‚Äì[3.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS4 "3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose").
In particular, admissible trading strategies are bounded from below, filtrations are
required to satisfy the usual conditions, and asset prices are semimartingales relative to
a reference filtration.
Dynamic completeness is understood relative to both a filtration and admissibility.
It is invoked here solely to characterize existence of a global martingale pricing measure
for asset prices, and not to introduce portfolio-based pricing objects.
Given an admissible filtration ‚Ñç\mathbb{H} and a probability measure
‚Ñö‚àº‚Ñô\mathbb{Q}\sim\mathbb{P}, the market is said to be *dynamically complete under
(‚Ñç,‚Ñö)(\mathbb{H},\mathbb{Q})* if every square-integrable ‚Ñç\mathbb{H}-martingale admits a
representation as a stochastic integral with respect to the asset price process SS using
admissible strategies. The following theorem establishes the equivalence between global martingale pricing and
admissible dynamic completeness.

###### Theorem 3.21 (Equivalence with admissible dynamic completeness).

The following statements are equivalent:

1. 1.

   There exists an admissible pricing structure (‚Ñç,‚Ñö)(\mathbb{H},\mathbb{Q}) such that the
   entire asset price vector SS is a local (‚Ñö,‚Ñç)(\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{H})-martingale.
2. 2.

   There exists an admissible filtration ‚Ñç\mathbb{H} and a probability measure
   ‚Ñö‚àº‚Ñô\mathbb{Q}\sim\mathbb{P} under which the market is dynamically complete.

###### Proof 3.22.

Suppose (1) holds.
If SS is a local (‚Ñö,‚Ñç)(\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{H})-martingale and the market is dynamically complete
under (‚Ñç,‚Ñö)(\mathbb{H},\mathbb{Q}), then standard martingale representation results imply that
every ‚Ñç\mathbb{H}-martingale can be expressed as a stochastic integral with respect to
SS.
Hence (2) holds.

Conversely, suppose (2) holds.
Dynamic completeness implies uniqueness of the equivalent local martingale measure under
‚Ñç\mathbb{H} and ensures that all asset prices admit martingale representations under
(‚Ñö,‚Ñç)(\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{H}).
Hence (1) holds.

Theorem¬†[3.21](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem21 "Theorem 3.21 (Equivalence with admissible dynamic completeness). ‚Ä£ 3.5 Dynamic Completeness and Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") shows that the obstruction identified in
Section¬†[3.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.SS4 "3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") is not a failure of martingale pricing in general, but a
manifestation of failure of admissible dynamic completeness.
When admissible trading strategies span all sources of uncertainty present in the
filtration, a global equivalent martingale measure exists and the obstruction disappears.
Conversely, when admissible dynamic completeness fails, no single admissible filtration can
support martingale pricing for all assets simultaneously.

This equivalence highlights a structural distinction between classical formulations and
the present setting.
In standard results, dynamic completeness is studied relative to a fixed filtration.
Here, the filtration itself is constrained by admissibility and time-ordering, and the
obstruction arises because no admissible filtration supports completeness once multiple
unspanned sources of uncertainty are present.

The results of this paper therefore identify a sharp boundary for global martingale pricing
within the classical framework.
They show that local and pairwise martingale pricing may be compatible with admissibility
even when global pricing is not, and that this failure is equivalent to the impossibility
of achieving admissible dynamic completeness.
No claims are made beyond this probabilistic boundary, and the results do not preclude
alternative modeling approaches outside the martingale pricing framework.

The analysis shows that no-arbitrage pricing imposes strong constraints on admissible causal
reasoning.
Causality compatible with pricing is inherently filtration-dependent and may fail to admit
a global representation under realistic institutional constraints.
The results clarify the limits of aggregation in asset pricing and provide a unified
interpretation of market segmentation, regime dependence, and the instability of pricing
relations in incomplete markets.

## 5 Conclusions and Future Work

This paper studies no-arbitrage asset pricing when the information structure is treated as an
endogenous object constrained by admissibility and time-ordering, rather than as an exogenously
fixed primitive. For any finite group of traded assets, we show that whenever martingale pricing
is feasible under some admissible filtration, it is already feasible under a canonical reduced
filtration generated by the asset prices themselves. These pricing-sufficient filtrations are
minimal, unique up to null sets, and stable under restriction of the asset set and under pairwise
aggregation, as established in
Propositions¬†[3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem3 "Proposition 3.3 (Canonical local reduction of information). ‚Ä£ 3.2 Local Existence and Stability of Pricing-Sufficient Filtrations ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose"),
[3.6](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem6 "Proposition 3.6 (Stability under restriction). ‚Ä£ 3.2 Local Existence and Stability of Pricing-Sufficient Filtrations ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose"), and¬†[3.8](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem8 "Proposition 3.8 (Pairwise compatibility under a common pricing measure). ‚Ä£ 3.2 Local Existence and Stability of Pricing-Sufficient Filtrations ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose").

The main theoretical contribution is the identification of a sharp structural obstruction to
global martingale pricing. Under admissibility and time-ordering constraints, and in the presence
of multiple independent unspanned finite-variation drivers, there exists no admissible filtration
and no equivalent probability measure under which all admissible asset prices are jointly local
martingales. The obstruction is minimal: global pricing remains feasible with a single unspanned
driver; pairwise compatibility can be maintained with two independent drivers; and failure arises
for the first time with three independent drivers. As shown in
Theorem¬†[3.19](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem19 "Theorem 3.19 (Minimal obstruction to global martingale pricing). ‚Ä£ 3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") and
Theorem¬†[3.21](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem21 "Theorem 3.21 (Equivalence with admissible dynamic completeness). ‚Ä£ 3.5 Dynamic Completeness and Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose"), this non-existence result is equivalent to the failure
of admissible dynamic completeness, placing the obstruction squarely within the classical
no-arbitrage framework.

These results clarify a limitation not addressed by standard incompleteness theory. Classical
formulations analyze incompleteness relative to a fixed filtration and attribute non-uniqueness of
equivalent martingale measures to lack of spanning. Here, the obstruction arises at a more
primitive level. Even when each finite asset group admits a well-defined equivalent local
martingale measure and pairwise pricing consistency holds, admissibility and non-anticipativity
may prevent the existence of any single filtration supporting joint martingale pricing.

The numerical illustrations are intended solely as constructive diagnostics consistent with the
theory. Using a controlled simulated environment and discrete-time Doob‚ÄìMeyer decompositions,
they visualize how admissible information structures induce only negligible predictable
finite-variation components, while inadmissible enlargements of the filtration (violating
admissibility or time-ordering) generate systematic predictability. The simulations do not test
the theoretical results, but illustrate the mechanism underlying the obstruction in
Theorem¬†[3.19](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem19 "Theorem 3.19 (Minimal obstruction to global martingale pricing). ‚Ä£ 3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose").

#### Limitations and scope

Several limitations should be emphasized. The paper does not claim that global martingale measures
fail to exist in general, nor that markets are inherently incomplete; the impossibility result is
conditional on admissibility and time-ordering constraints and disappears under admissible dynamic
completeness. The analysis is independent of equilibrium considerations, and any causal language
is strictly interpretative. Finally, the numerical illustrations are not intended as empirical
validation, but as concrete examples illustrating the structural mechanisms identified in the
theory.

Future research directions include incorporating additional market frictions, such as transaction
costs or funding constraints, and extending the analysis to infinite-dimensional settings,
including term-structure models or markets with a continuum of assets. More broadly, the results
highlight that information, admissibility, and pricing are inseparable in arbitrage-free markets:
notions of dependence compatible with pricing must respect admissible trading and time-ordering
constraints, and may fail to admit a global representation even when local consistency is
preserved.

## Appendix E Admissibility Violations and Non-Anticipativity

This appendix completes the proof of Theorem¬†[3.19](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#S3.Thmtheorem19 "Theorem 3.19 (Minimal obstruction to global martingale pricing). ‚Ä£ 3.4 A Minimal Obstruction to Global Martingale Pricing ‚Ä£ 3 Main Results ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") by showing that any

The violation arises through predictability of future increments and is independent of
preferences or equilibrium considerations.

###### Remark E.1 (Scope of the triple-conditioning argument).

Appendix¬†[E](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.12541v1#A5 "Appendix E Admissibility Violations and Non-Anticipativity ‚Ä£ Admissible Information Structures and the Non-Existence of Global Martingale PricingSubmitted to the editors 17/01/2026. \fundingThere is no funding to disclose") establishes
  
non-admissibility of joint conditioning on three independent unspanned slow drivers by means of an explicit stylized construction. The argument does not claim that any arbitrary triple of finite-variation processes induces predictability, but rather that there exist economically natural and probabilistically well-defined environments in which triple conditioning necessarily violates non-anticipativity under time-scale separation. The result is therefore constructive rather than universal.

### E.1 Non-anticipativity and admissibility

Recall that admissible trading strategies must be predictable with respect to the pricing
filtration.
A filtration is admissible only if future innovations of driving processes are not
predictable relative to that filtration. Formally, let XX be a semimartingale.
A filtration ùîæ\mathbb{G} is admissible for XX if the compensator of XX relative to
ùîæ\mathbb{G} does not contain information about future increments of XX.

### E.2 Triple conditioning induces predictability

Recall that a market is dynamically complete if every contingent claim measurable with

###### Definition F.1 (Admissible dynamic completeness).

## Appendix G Pricing-Consistent Causality and Causal Frameworks

This appendix compares the notion of pricing-consistent causality implicit in the main text
with standard notions of causality used in statistics and econometrics.
The purpose is not to propose an alternative causal framework, but to clarify which forms
of causal reasoning are compatible with no-arbitrage pricing under admissibility.

### G.1 Pricing-consistent causality

Throughout the paper, causality is not postulated as a primitive concept.
Instead, it is derived from invariance properties of valuation operators under admissible
changes to information structures.

Formally, let ùîæ\mathbb{G} be a minimal pricing-sufficient filtration for an asset group AA,
and let ùí¨‚Äã(ùîæ)\mathcal{Q}(\mathbb{G}) denote the corresponding set of equivalent local martingale
measures. A transformation of an economic variable is said to be *pricing-consistent* if it
induces invariant pricing kernels across all measures in ùí¨‚Äã(ùîæ)\mathcal{Q}(\mathbb{G}). Only such transformations admit causal interpretation within the pricing framework.
All statements are conditional on the filtration and admissibility constraints.

### G.2 Pearl-style structural causal models

Structural causal models as developed by Pearl [pearl2009causality] define
causality through counterfactual interventions implemented via the do‚Äã(‚ãÖ)\texttt{do}(\cdot)
operator.
An intervention replaces a structural equation while holding others fixed.

In financial markets, such interventions are generally incompatible with pricing.
Replacing the law of a price process, volatility, or funding variable while holding trading
opportunities fixed typically violates self-financing or admissibility constraints.
As a result, the martingale property is destroyed and no equivalent martingale measure
exists under the intervened system. Thus, Pearl-style interventions are not admissible operations in the sense of
no-arbitrage pricing.
The incompatibility is structural and does not depend on equilibrium assumptions.

### G.3 Granger causality

Granger causality [granger1969investigating] defines causality through predictability:
a process XX Granger-causes YY if past values of XX improve prediction of YY relative to a given filtration. This notion is purely predictive and filtration-dependent. It is compatible with pricing-consistent causality but strictly weaker.
Predictive relationships may exist that have no pricing relevance, and conversely,
pricing-relevant variables may fail to Granger-cause prices if their effects are absorbed into the pricing kernel. Granger causality therefore captures temporal dependence, not structural invariance under admissible changes.

### G.4 Structural equation models in econometrics

Econometric structural models often specify systems of stochastic equations with
exogenous shocks and identify causal effects through exclusion restrictions or instruments [imbens2015causal].

Such models typically assume a fixed probability space and allow counterfactual variation of structural parameters. In contrast, asset pricing constrains probability measures through no-arbitrage and admissibility.
Structural variations that violate these constraints are not meaningful within the pricing framework. Accordingly, pricing-consistent causality is more restrictive than standard structural causality but applies in settings where prices and information are endogenously linked. The distinctions can be summarized as follows:

* ‚Ä¢

  Pearl SCMs allow arbitrary counterfactual interventions; pricing-consistent causality
  allows only admissible transformations preserving martingale pricing.
* ‚Ä¢

  Granger causality captures predictability, not invariance.
* ‚Ä¢

  Structural econometric models fix probability spaces ex ante; pricing endogenizes the
  probability measure through no-arbitrage.

As a result, causal reasoning compatible with asset pricing is necessarily local,
filtration-dependent, and constrained by admissibility.
This limitation is not a deficiency of causal modeling, but a structural feature of
no-arbitrage markets.
