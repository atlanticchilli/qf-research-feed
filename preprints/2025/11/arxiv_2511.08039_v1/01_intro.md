###### Abstract

Neoclassical economic theory presents marginal productivity (MP)
theory using the scalar notion of marginal products, and takes pains,
implicitly or explicitly, to show that competitive equilibrium satisfies the
supposedly ethical principle: â€œTo each what he and the
instruments he owns produces.â€ This paper shows that MP
theory can also be formulated in a mathematically equivalent way using
vectorial marginal productsâ€“which however conflicts with the above-mentioned
â€œdistributive sharesâ€ picture. Vectorial MP
theory also facilitates the presentation of modern treatment of the labor theory of property which on
the descriptive side is based on the fact that, contrary to the distributive
shares picture, one legal party gets the production vector consisting of 100
percent of the liabilities for the used-up inputs and 100 percent of the
produced outputs in a productive opportunity. On the normative side, the labor theory of property is just the application of the usual juridical norm of
imputation to the question of property appropriation.

marginal productivity theory, property theory, imputation of

## 1 Introduction

When many economists and philosophers consider the principle of people getting
the fruits of their labor, they will usually interpret it in terms of marginal
productivity. John Rawls is a good example.

> Accepting the marginal productivity theory of distribution, each
> factor of production receives an income according to how much it adds to
> output (assuming private property in the means of production). In this sense,
> a worker is paid the full value of the results of his labor, no more and no
> less. Offhand this strikes us as fair. It appeals to a traditional idea of the
> natural right of property in the fruits of our labor. Therefore to some
> writers the precept of contribution has seemed satisfactory as a principle of
> justice. [[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08039v1#bib.bib26), p. 271]

The neoclassical claim is that under the conditions of competitive
equilibrium, each unit of labor â€œgets what it
produces.â€ Indeed, Milton Friedman calls it the
â€œcapitalist ethicâ€ [[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08039v1#bib.bib15), p. 164]:

> of income in a free market society is, â€œTo each what he and
> the instruments he owns produces.â€ [[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08039v1#bib.bib15), pp. 161-2]

Well-meaning liberal, progressive, and even heterodox economists emphasize
that the actual economy may be neither competitive nor in equilibrium, that
fixed proportions in production (Sraffa) raise enormous difficulties in
measuring the marginal product of each factor of production, and that there
may be nothing just in the given distribution of wealth. But they raise no
objection in principle to the interpretation of marginal productivity theory
as giving people â€œwhat they produceâ€; they

### 3.1 The Metaphor: Only persons have responsible agency

The first and foremost problem is the neglect of the difference between
responsible human actions and the non-responsible but causally efficacious
(i.e., productive) services of things like a wrench, machine, or truck. This
fundamental distinction between persons and things in terms of imputation is
part of standard jurisprudence.

> A person is the subject whose actions are susceptible to imputation.
> â€¦ A thing is something that is not susceptible to imputation.
> [[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08039v1#bib.bib19), pp. 24-25]

The distinction is hardly new or subtle except perhaps in orthodox economics

> We have insisted that the word â€œproduceâ€ in the sense of the specific [i.e., marginal]
> productivity theory of distribution, is used in precisely the same way as the

> â€œproductive resources.â€ [[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.08039v1#bib.bib21), p.

### 3.3 The Miracle: Virgin birth of marginal products

There is still another flaw in the orthodox treatment of MP theory. The
ideological baggage being carried by MP theory forces it to be presented in a
factually implausible way, i.e., the â€œvirgin
birthâ€ of marginal products. This factually implausible part
of the orthodox view is the picture of a unit of a factor as â€œimmaculatelyâ€ producing its marginal product without using
up any other inputs. When another unit of an input xx is used, then by

process, more output can be produced using the same amount of the other
factors, and that extra output is the â€œmarginal
productâ€ of that xx factor. But cost-minimizing firms would
not make such a hypothetical shift when increasing factor usage; they would
expand along the least-cost expansion path. More of other factors must then be
used, so the additional factors and product would be given by a vector, a
vectorial marginal product. The simple â€œdistributive
shares of the productâ€ framing collapses since the vectorial
marginal product includes both a â€œshareâ€ of
the output but also an increase in using up other inputs to expand along the
least-cost expansion path.

In this essay, we give the mathematically equivalent vectorial presentation of
MP theory, which is based on the more plausible picture that a unit of labor
can only produce more of the outputs by using up more of the other inputs at
minimum cost. The â€œproblemâ€ with this
version of MP theory is that it does not lend itself to the ideologically
appealing picture of each unit of a factor as â€œproducing its
marginal product.â€ Thus we have a central example about how
the ideological baggage being towed by orthodox economics affects even the
mathematical presentation of the standard theories.

The problem (or internal incoherence) in the usual treatment is simply that a

Before turning to the vectorial treatment of marginal products, we must remove

produced ex nihilo by the extra unit of labor.

The fundamental question about production is not about distributive

Nothing is produced ex nihilo. Labor cannot produce tractors without

considered symmetrically in a vectorial form.

### A.5 MP Theory Without Substitution

There is a school of heterodox economics (the neo-Ricardian school associated
with Sraffa and to some extent Marx) that dismisses marginal productivity theory by assuming fixed
proportions and thus disputing the assumption of substitutability.

We have criticized the usual interpretation of Mâ€‹PiMP\_{i} as the
